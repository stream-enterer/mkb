# CDDA Survival Systems → 1840s Paris Medical Ontology

> A framework for refluffing CDDA's survival mechanics through the lens of 1840s Parisian medicine.
> This is **not** a 1:1 rename. The period's understanding carved reality into different categories
> than modern biology. Systems merge, split, gain competing explanations, and sometimes simply
> vanish into gaps no practitioner could see.

---

## Part I: The Interpretive Landscape

### 1.1 Who Practiced Medicine in 1840s Paris?

The city contained a **contested ecology** of healers. No single framework monopolized understanding.

| Practitioner | Fr. Term | Training | Framework | Legal Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **University physician** (*médecin*) | *docteur en médecine* | 6 years, Faculty of Medicine | Anatomo-clinical method; eclecticism | Fully licensed |
| **Health officer** | *officier de santé* | 3 years or apprenticeship | Simplified academic medicine | Licensed, restricted scope (no major surgery, rural emphasis) |
| **Surgeon** | *chirurgien* | Faculty, post-1794 merged with medicine | Operative; anatomo-pathological | Fully licensed |
| **Pharmacist / Apothecary** | *pharmacien* | Faculty of Pharmacy | Materia medica, chemical compounding | Licensed for dispensing |
| **Midwife** | *sage-femme* | Hospital training (Maternité) | Obstetric empiricism | Licensed for childbirth |
| **Homeopath** | *homéopathe* | Variable; Hahnemann died in Paris 1843 | "Like cures like," infinitesimal doses | Tolerated, legally grey |
| **Hydropathist** | *hydropathe* | Self-trained; Priessnitz method | Water cure (*Wasserheilkunde*) | No license; quasi-legal |
| **Magnetizer / Mesmerist** | *magnétiseur* | Apprenticeship/self-study | Animal magnetism (*magnétisme animal*); magnetic fluid | **Banned** by Académie de Médecine, 1842. Baron Du Potet continues underground via *Journal du magnétisme* (1845–61). Braid in Britain reframes it as "hypnotism" (physiological, not magnetic). |
| **Raspailist** | *raspailliste* | Self-treatment via published manuals | Parasitic theory of disease; camphor as universal remedy | Explicitly illegal (Raspail practiced *médecine illégale*) |
| **Herbalist** | *herboriste* | Guild-like apprenticeship | Plant-based empiricism | Licensed with state diploma from Faculty of Pharmacy (1803–1941). Distinct from and often in conflict with *pharmaciens*. |
| **Bone-setter** | *rebouteux* / *rhabilleur* | Hereditary/apprenticeship | Manual manipulation, folk anatomy | Illegal but tolerated rurally |
| **Catholic healer** | *guérisseur religieux* | None formal; parish affiliation | Saints, relics, prayer, holy water, pilgrimage | Not regulated (religious freedom) |
| **Marabout** (Maghrebi) | *marabout* | Quranic schools; Sufi lineage | *Baraka* (divine grace), Prophetic medicine (*al-tibb al-nabawi*), Galenic-Arabic tradition | Illegal under French law; practiced in Algerian diaspora |
| **Jewish practitioner** | — | Variable; German-Jewish often university-trained | Academic medicine + negotiation with *halakhic* dietary/bodily laws | If licensed, legal |
| **Charlatan / Quack** | *charlatan*, *empirique* | None | Patent remedies (*remèdes secrets*), fairground spectacle | Illegal, widespread |
| **Folk healer** | *guérisseur*, *sorcier* | Oral tradition, family lineage | Sympathetic magic, herbal lore, *signatures* | Illegal |

### 1.2 Legal Framework

The law of **19 Ventôse Year XI** (10 March 1803) established the medical monopoly:
- Only *docteurs en médecine* and *officiers de santé* could legally practice medicine
- *Herboristes* received a separate state diploma
- *Pharmaciens* had their own licensing
- **All other healing = illegal** (*exercice illégal de la médecine*)
- Enforcement was weak, especially outside Paris. The 1845 *Congrès Médical de Paris* specifically highlighted the need to combat illegal practice.

### 1.3 Dominant Academic Frameworks (1840s)

| Framework | Key Figures | Core Claim | Status c.1845 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anatomo-clinical method** (*méthode anatomo-clinique*) | Laënnec (d.1826), Louis, Andral, Trousseau, Bouillaud | Disease = localized lesion; diagnose at bedside, confirm at autopsy. Motto: *"Lisez peu, voyez beaucoup"* ("Read little, see a lot"). | **Dominant** in hospital teaching, but reaching its conceptual limits. German laboratory science beginning to surpass Paris. |
| **Numerical method** (*méthode numérique*) | Pierre-Charles-Alexandre Louis | Statistical evidence over authority; count cases | Rising; controversial |
| **Physiological medicine** (*médecine physiologique*) | Broussais (d.1838) | All disease = gastric inflammation (*gastro-entérite*); treat with leeches. His journal (*Annales de la médecine physiologique*) ceased 1834. | **Dead** — discredited by 1832 cholera (his leech treatments killed patients) and by Louis's statistics. But leeching as a practice lingers. |
| **Vitalism** (*vitalisme*) | Bichat (d.1802), Montpellier school (Barthez, Bordeu) | Life = irreducible vital properties (*sensibilité*, *contractilité*); not reducible to physics/chemistry | Still influential, especially outside Paris |
| **Experimental physiology** (*physiologie expérimentale*) | Magendie, Claude Bernard (from 1841) | Vivisection, controlled experiment, chemical analysis of body functions | **Emerging**; radical fringe becoming mainstream |
| **Chemical/organic analysis** | Liebig (*Die Thierchemie*, 1842) | Food = plastic (nitrogenous) + respiratory (non-nitrogenous) substances; body = combustion engine | **Cutting edge**; known to educated physicians |
| **Humoralism** (*théorie humorale*) | Hippocrates/Galen legacy | Four humors (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile); disease = imbalance | **Officially dead** in faculties; **alive** in folk practice and patient self-understanding |
| **Miasma theory** (*théorie des miasmes*) | Widely held | Disease from corrupted air (*mauvais air*), swamp emanations, filth | **Consensus** for epidemic disease; no germ theory yet |

### 1.4 What Patients Believed

Regardless of whom they consulted, most Parisians operated with a **folk synthesis**:

- The body has a *constitution* (*tempérament*) that is innately strong or weak
- Illness comes from bad air, chills, moral shock, or dietary excess
- Blood can be "too thick" or "too thin"; needs periodic release
- The stomach is the seat of health; digestion is paramount
- "Strength" (*force*) is a finite resource depleted by exertion, restored by food and rest
- Emotions directly cause physical disease
- Some illnesses require a priest, not a doctor

## Part II: Ontological Remapping

### 2.1 How 1840s Paris Carves the Body Differently Than CDDA

CDDA models ~12 distinct interacting systems. The 1840s does not recognize these as separate. Below: which CDDA systems get **merged**, which get **split**, and which fall into **conceptual gaps**.

#### Mergers (CDDA systems that 1840s collapses together)

| CDDA Systems | 1840s Unified Concept | Fr. Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hunger + Thirst + Weight + Vitamins | **Nutrition / Nourishment** | *alimentation*, *nutrition* | No calorie concept. No vitamin concept. All dietary effects = one domain: "how well nourished is the patient?" Observable via corpulence, complexion, energy. |
| Stamina + Weariness | **Vital Force / Nervous Energy** | *force vitale*, *énergie nerveuse* | Short-term and long-term exhaustion are not distinguished mechanically. Both = depletion of the same animating force. |
| Sleep + Sleepiness + Sleep Deprivation | **Nervous Exhaustion** | *épuisement nerveux*, *insomnie* | Sleep, fatigue, and deprivation all attributed to the nervous system. No separate "sleepiness counter" vs "deprivation timer." |
| Morale + Stimulants (partially) | **Passions / Moral State** | *passions de l'âme*, *état moral* | Emotions, stimulant effects on mood, and mental state form one category of "moral causes" (*causes morales*) affecting health. |

#### Splits (CDDA systems that 1840s divides more finely)

| CDDA System | 1840s Subdivisions | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Digestion Pipeline** (stomach → guts → body) | 1. *Coction* / stomach digestion (*digestion stomacale*) | The stomach dissolves food into *chyme* |
| | 2. *Chylification* / intestinal digestion | Intestines extract *chyle* (nutritive fluid) |
| | 3. *Sanguification* / blood-making (*hématose*) | Chyle enters blood; blood itself is "made" in this step |
| | 4. *Assimilation* / tissue-building | Blood becomes flesh, fat, bone — a distinct vital process |
| **Body Temperature** | 1. *Chaleur animale* (animal heat) — internal generation | Lavoisier's "combustion in the lungs"; a chemical/vital process |
| | 2. *Impressions extérieures* — environmental exposure | Cold, heat, wind — external agents acting on the body |
| | 3. *Fièvre* (fever) — a disease entity, **not** a temperature reading | Fever is a named condition with its own taxonomy (see §3.9) |
| **Morale** | 1. *Tempérament* — innate disposition (sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic) | Permanent; you're born with it |
| | 2. *Passions* — acute emotional states | Transient; directly cause illness |
| | 3. *Hygiène morale* — moral lifestyle/habits | Modifiable; long-term behavioral health |

#### Conceptual Gaps (things CDDA tracks that 1840s cannot see)

| CDDA Concept | Gap Type | What 1840s Sees Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Calories** (numerical energy unit) | **Total gap.** The calorie entered dictionaries as a heat unit by 1840 but was never applied to food/nutrition until the 1890s (Atwater). | "Nourishing power" (*pouvoir nutritif*) — qualitative, not quantitative. Liebig (1842) classifies foods as *plastic* vs *respiratory* but provides no numerical accounting. |
| **Vitamins** (specific micronutrients) | **Total gap.** Not isolated until 1910s–1930s. | Deficiency diseases (scurvy, rickets) are known empirically. Scurvy → citrus cure known since 1700s, but *why* is a mystery. Attributed to: constitution, bad air, dietary monotony, moral depression, or all at once. |
| **BMR** (basal metabolic rate as calculable number) | **Total gap.** | "Vital expenditure" (*dépense vitale*) is vaguely understood. Lavoisier showed respiration = combustion (1780s). Bernard will quantify aspects in the 1850s. But no numerical rate exists. |
| **Precise body temperature** (per-body-part, in degrees) | **Practical gap.** Clinical thermometry not standard until Wunderlich (1860s). | Physicians assess temperature by touch (*chaleur au toucher*). A fever is diagnosed by pulse rate, skin feel, and patient report — not a number. |
| **Parasites in food** (as specific organisms) | **Partial gap.** Intestinal worms are well known. Bacteria are not. | Worms (*vers intestinaux*) are treated empirically (vermifuges). Microscopic pathogens are invisible; food poisoning is attributed to "putrefaction" (*putréfaction*) or miasma. Raspail, eccentric though he is, actually invokes "parasites" (*parasites*) as a unified disease theory — closer to germ theory than the mainstream. |
| **Weariness as separate from stamina** | **Conceptual gap.** | Both are "exhaustion" (*épuisement*). The distinction between short-burst capacity and cumulative fatigue debt is not formalized. |

### 2.2 The Constitution Meta-System

The concept that does the most work in 1840s medicine — and has **no single CDDA equivalent** — is **constitution** (*constitution* or *tempérament*).

Constitution is a **meta-stat** that encompasses what CDDA splits into: Lifestyle, base stats, mutation traits, and starting health.

| Aspect | Fr. Term | Maps To (CDDA) |
|---|---|---|
| Innate bodily type | *tempérament* (sanguine/choleric/melancholic/phlegmatic) | Character creation traits |
| Innate strength/weakness | *constitution forte / faible* | Base stats, starting health |
| Acquired health state | *habitude du corps* | Lifestyle stat, accumulated effects |
| Susceptibility to disease | *prédisposition* | Mutation traits, vitamin levels |
| Outward signs | *habitus extérieur* | Weight category, visible effects |

A physician examining a patient would assess constitution **first**, before considering any specific ailment. This is the diagnostic entry point.

## Part III: System-by-System Translation

### 3.1 Digestion Pipeline

**CDDA Model:** Stomach (2500 mL) → Guts (24 L) → Body storage. Tracked numerically with rates.

**1840s Understanding:**

| Stage | CDDA | Academic (*Faculté*) | Vitalist | Folk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ingestion** | Food enters stomach; volume computed | Food enters the *estomac*; too much causes *indigestion* | Vital force guides the stomach's action | "The stomach receives" |
| **Stomach processing** | Solids dissolve at capacity/6 per 30 min | Gastric juice (*suc gastrique*) dissolves food into *chyme*. Beaumont's experiments (1833) known — gastric juice works in vitro. Magendie confirms chemical nature. | Dissolution requires vital force; chemistry alone is insufficient | "The stomach cooks the food" (*l'estomac cuit les aliments*) |
| **Transfer to intestines** | Stomach → Guts at fixed rate | *Chyme* passes to the *intestin grêle* (small intestine); bile and pancreatic juice act on it. Bernard discovers pancreatic lipase (1848). | Same anatomical pathway, different explanatory cause | "Food passes through the bowels" |
| **Nutrient absorption** | Guts absorb calories/vitamins to body | Intestinal villi extract *chyle* — a milky nutritive fluid that enters the *vaisseaux chylifères* (lacteals) and then the blood | Chyle is the vehicle of vital nourishment | "Good food makes good blood" |
| **Body storage** | stored_calories, vitamin_levels | Chyle → blood (*sanguification* / *hématose*) → flesh (*assimilation*). Fat = excess nutritive material deposited. | Blood is "vivified" in the lungs by contact with air | "You are what you eat" — nearly literal |

**Key differences:**
- No numerical tracking. "Well nourished" vs "poorly nourished" is qualitative.
- The **four-stage pipeline** (coction → chylification → sanguification → assimilation) is richer than CDDA's two-compartment model, but less mechanistically precise.
- **Vomiting**: 1840s understands it as *sympathetic* response (stomach nerves reacting) or *inflammation*. CDDA models it as capacity overflow.
- **Drug absorption**: 1840s has no concept of drugs bypassing the gut. All ingested substances go through the same pipeline. Alcohol's rapid effects are attributed to its "stimulating" nature, not absorption mechanics.

**Practitioner variations:**

| Practitioner | Digestive Theory |
|---|---|
| Broussais followers | All digestive problems = *gastro-entérite* (stomach inflammation). Treat with leeches on the abdomen. |
| Homeopaths | Digestive symptoms are expressions of the whole organism's *psora* (Hahnemann's miasmatic theory). Treat with infinitesimal doses of substances that cause similar symptoms. |
| Raspailists | All digestive problems = parasites. Treat with camphor cigarettes, camphor lozenges, camphorated alcohol. |
| Marabouts (Maghrebi) | Stomach ailments from *'ayn* (evil eye) or *jinn* possession. Treat with Quranic recitation (*ruqya*), herbal preparations from Prophetic medicine. |
| Folk healers | Stomach "cold" or "blockage." Treat with herbal tisanes (*tisanes*): chamomile, mint, sage. |

### 3.2 Hunger System

**CDDA Model:** Derived display state from stomach fullness + caloric deficit + BMI. States: Engorged → Full → Satisfied → (blank) → Hungry → Very Hungry → Famished → Near Starving → Starving.

**1840s Understanding:**

Hunger (*faim*) is straightforward sensory experience. What is **not** understood:
- That hunger is driven by caloric deficit (calories don't exist as a nutritional concept)
- That stored body fat relates to hunger perception
- The distinction between "stomach fullness sensation" and "actual nutritive need"

| CDDA State | 1840s Equivalent | Fr. Term | How Understood |
|---|---|---|---|
| Engorged | Surfeit / Repletion | *réplétion*, *satiété excessive* | Dangerous excess; risks *indigestion*, *apoplexie* (stroke). Moralized as *intempérance* (gluttony). |
| Full | Satiety | *satiété* | The stomach signals enough. A pleasant, desired state. |
| Satisfied | Comfort | *bien-être* | Recent meal, no distress. |
| (blank) | Normal | — | Not medically relevant. |
| Hungry | Appetite | *appétit*, *faim* | Natural signal. Healthy appetite = sign of good constitution. "Appetite is the best sauce." |
| Very Hungry | Strong hunger | *grand'faim* | The stomach "calls for nourishment" (*réclame la nourriture*). Gastric irritation theory: empty stomach generates acid that irritates its own walls. |
| Famished | Inanition | *inanition* | Dangerous nutritive depletion. Medical emergency if prolonged. |
| Near Starving | Marasmus / Consumption | *marasme*, *consomption* | Wasting disease. Often conflated with tuberculosis (*phtisie*). The patient "consumes himself." |
| Starving | Famine / Death by starvation | *famine*, *mort d'inanition* | Well documented from Irish Famine (1845–52) reports reaching Paris. Understood as total depletion of the body's substance. |

**Critical difference:** CDDA separates *sensation* (stomach fullness) from *reality* (stored calories). 1840s collapses them. If the stomach is full, the patient is nourished. If the patient is thin, the stomach has been "insufficient." The idea that a fat person could feel hungry (because stored calories are fine but stomach is empty) exists as folk observation but has no medical framework.

### 3.3 Calorie & Weight System

**CDDA Model:** stored_calories (millikcal), healthy_calories, BMR (2500 kcal/day), activity multipliers (0.85x–10x), BMI categories, 7716 kcal/kg fat.

**1840s Replacement Concept: Nourishing Power & Corpulence**

There is **no calorie**. The replacement is a qualitative hierarchy:

| Modern Concept | 1840s Equivalent | What They Knew | What They Didn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| Calories | *Pouvoir nutritif* (nourishing power) | Some foods are "more nourishing" than others. Meat > vegetables. Bread = staff of life. | No numbers. No energy accounting. No way to compare foods quantitatively. |
| Macronutrients | Liebig's two classes (1842) | **Plastic foods** (*aliments plastiques* / nitrogenous): meat, eggs, cheese → build tissue. **Respiratory foods** (*aliments respiratoires* / non-nitrogenous): sugar, starch, fat → fuel body heat. | This is cutting-edge and not widely adopted by practitioners. Most doctors don't use Liebig's system. |
| BMR | *Dépense vitale* (vital expenditure) | The body "burns" material to maintain heat (Lavoisier, 1780s). Activity requires more "combustion." | No numerical rate. No way to measure or calculate it. |
| Activity multipliers | — | Hard labor requires more food than sedentary life. Soldiers need more rations. | Purely empirical observation, not a multiplier framework. |
| Body fat storage | *Embonpoint* (plumpness), *obésité* | Fat deposits from excess nourishment. Widely observed. | No concept of kcal-per-kg conversion. |
| BMI | **Quetelet Index** (1835!) | Adolphe Quetelet (*Sur l'homme et le développement de ses facultés*, 1835) published weight/height² as a statistical tool for the "average man" (*l'homme moyen*). | This is **statistical social physics**, not clinical medicine. No doctor uses it to assess patients. Purely academic. |

**Weight categories as understood:**

| CDDA Category | 1840s Term | Fr. Term | Medical Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emaciated | Cachexia / Marasmus | *cachexie*, *marasme* | Grave sign. "The body devours itself." Associated with chronic disease (tuberculosis, cancer). |
| Underweight | Thinness / Lean | *maigreur* | May be constitutional (melancholic *tempérament*). Worrying if recent onset. |
| Normal | Good nourishment | *bon état de nutrition* | "Well-constituted" (*bien constitué*). |
| Overweight | Plumpness | *embonpoint* | **Desirable** in this era. Sign of prosperity and good health. The sanguine *tempérament*. |
| Obese | Obesity / Corpulence | *obésité*, *polysarcie* | Medical concern. Associated with apoplexy (stroke), gout, breathing difficulty. But still less stigmatized than today. |
| Very Obese / Morbidly Obese | Monstrous corpulence | *obésité monstrueuse* | Rare case-report territory. |

**Key difference:** 1840s sees *embonpoint* (plumpness) as **healthy**, not as a penalty. CDDA penalizes overweight; 1840s rewards it. The reversal reflects actual period values.

### 3.4 Vitamin / Micronutrient System

**CDDA Model:** Per-vitamin counters with min/max, deficiency/excess effects, severity tiers. Types: VITAMIN, TOXIN, DRUG, COUNTER.

**1840s: The Invisible System**

Vitamins are **completely unknown**. But their *effects* are observed:

| CDDA Vitamin | Deficiency Disease | 1840s Name | Fr. Term | 1840s Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `vitC` | Scurvy | Scurvy | *scorbut* | **Best understood.** Dietary origin known since James Lind (1753). Citrus cure well established in navies. But *why* citrus works = mystery. Academic: "anti-scorbutic principle" in fresh foods. Folk: fresh food has "vital quality" lost in preservation. |
| `iron` | Anemia | Chlorosis / Green sickness | *chlorose*, *anémie* | Common diagnosis in young women. Treated with iron preparations (*fer*) empirically — one of the great accidental successes. Andral's blood analysis (1840s) shows reduced blood cells. But no concept of iron as a specific nutrient. |
| `calcium` | Bone weakness / Rickets | Rickets (in children) | *rachitisme* | Attributed to: damp housing, insufficient sunlight, bad air, poor constitution. **Not linked to diet.** |
| `ethanol` | — (it's a drug) | Drunkenness / Alcoholism | *ivresse*, *alcoolisme* | Magnus Huss coins *alcoholismus chronicus* (1849). Well understood as dose-dependent. But conceptualized as a **moral failing** as much as a medical condition. |
| `BAC` | — | Blood alcohol | — | **No concept.** Blood alcohol levels unmeasurable. Drunkenness assessed by behavior. |
| `blood` | Hypovolemia | Blood loss / Exsanguination | *perte de sang*, *hémorragie* | **Well understood** — bleeding is the most visible medical crisis. Ironic: therapeutic bloodletting (*saignée*) is still practiced alongside horror at pathological hemorrhage. |
| `mutagen` | — | — | — | **No equivalent.** Mutations, genetics, DNA all unknown. |
| `caffeine` (drug) | — | Coffee/tea stimulation | *excitation* | Caffeine not isolated until 1820 (Runge). Effects attributed to the "stimulating principle" of coffee. Well known empirically. |

**The vitamin-type distinction collapses.** CDDA separates VITAMIN, TOXIN, DRUG, COUNTER. The 1840s recognizes only:
- **Nourishment** (*aliment*) — things that build the body
- **Medicine / Remedy** (*médicament*, *remède*) — things that alter the body's state
- **Poison** (*poison*) — things that destroy the body

These three categories replace the entire vitamin system. A substance's classification depends on dose and intent, not on biochemical type.

**The deficiency-disease link is empirical, not mechanistic:**

| What They Observed | What They Concluded | What Was Actually Happening |
|---|---|---|
| Sailors on long voyages get scurvy; fresh food prevents it | Fresh food contains an "anti-scorbutic principle" | Vitamin C |
| Iron pills help chlorotic women | Iron is a tonic for weak blood (*sang faible*) | Iron supplementation |
| Cod liver oil helps rickets | It is a nourishing tonic | Vitamin D |
| Varied diet prevents disease | Monotonous diet weakens constitution | Multiple micronutrient adequacy |

### 3.5 Thirst / Hydration System

**CDDA Model:** Integer -100 to 1200, display states, death at 1200, linear speed penalties.

**1840s Understanding:**

Thirst (*soif*) is well understood as a sensation. Dehydration as a **medical emergency** is understood primarily through the lens of **cholera** — Paris suffered devastating epidemics in 1832 and 1849.

| CDDA Concept | 1840s Equivalent | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Thirst counter | *Soif* — assessed qualitatively by patient report | No numerical tracking |
| Overhydrated (Turgid) | *Pléthore aqueuse* (watery plethora) | Rare concern; more worried about blood plethora |
| Normal range | Not medically relevant | Drinking when thirsty = sufficient |
| Thirsty → Very Thirsty | *Soif vive* (keen thirst) | Symptom of fever, diabetes (*diabète*), cholera |
| Dehydrated → Parched | *Dessèchement* (desiccation) | Cholera patients recognized as dying of fluid loss. Thomas Latta (1832) attempted intravenous saline in Edinburgh — with remarkable results, but Latta died in 1833 and the technique was **abandoned and forgotten**. By the 1849 Paris epidemic, "nothing had been learned about the disease since 1832." Proposed treatments at the Académie included electromagnetic insulation, tobacco, and wrapping patients in boiling-water-dipped sheets. |
| Death by dehydration | *Mort de soif* | Understood. Cholera kills partly by dehydration. But the *mechanism* (electrolyte loss, circulatory collapse) is not understood. |

**Humoral overlay:** Thirst also fits the hot/dry quadrant of humoral thinking. A "dry constitution" (*tempérament sec*) is naturally thirstier. Choleric and melancholic temperaments tend dry.

**Water quality matters enormously.** 1840s Paris has no water treatment. The Seine is the primary water source for much of the city. "Bad water" (*mauvaise eau*) is a recognized cause of illness — but through miasma theory, not germ theory.

| Practitioner | Thirst/Hydration Approach |
|---|---|
| Academic physician | Prescribes *tisanes* (herbal infusions), broths (*bouillons*), barley water (*eau d'orge*). Monitors thirst as fever symptom. |
| Hydropathist | Water is medicine itself. Cold water wraps, wet sheets, sitz baths. Internal: copious cold water drinking as cure. |
| Homeopath | Thirst modalities are diagnostic: "thirst for small sips" vs "thirst for large quantities" → different remedies. |
| Folk practice | Spring water (*eau de source*) > river water. Holy water (*eau bénite*) for spiritual ailments. |

### 3.6 Sleepiness / Sleep System

**CDDA Model:** Sleepiness counter (0–1000+), sleep deprivation timer (separate), comfort levels affecting recovery, microsleep/forced-sleep mechanics.

**1840s Concept: Nervous Exhaustion & the Restorative Power of Sleep**

| CDDA Element | 1840s Mapping | Fr. Term |
|---|---|---|
| Sleepiness counter | *Besoin de sommeil* (need for sleep) | Single concept, not a counter |
| Sleep deprivation timer | **Not distinguished** from sleepiness itself | — |
| Microsleeps | *Somnolence involontaire* | Recognized as symptom of extreme fatigue or disease |
| Forced sleep (passout at 1000) | *Syncope*, *défaillance* | Collapse from exhaustion. Well documented in military medicine. |
| Comfort levels | *Conditions de repos* | Recognized qualitatively: soft bed > hard ground > outdoors |
| Sleep recovery rate | — | Sleep "restores the nervous fluid" (*fluide nerveux*). Better conditions = better restoration. No multiplier. |

**Theoretical explanations for sleep (competing):**

| Theory | Proponent Type | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Nervous exhaustion | Academic (dominant) | Waking activity depletes *énergie nerveuse*. Sleep allows the brain to regenerate its force. |
| Vascular congestion | Some physicians | Blood accumulates in the brain during waking; sleep redistributes it. |
| Vital force restoration | Vitalists | The *force vitale* withdraws from the senses during sleep to focus on internal repair. |
| Magnetic fluid | Mesmerists | Sleep = natural state of magnetic equilibrium. Insomnia = magnetic imbalance. Induced "magnetic sleep" (*sommeil magnétique*) is therapeutic. |
| Divine rhythm | Religious | God ordained the day/night cycle for labor and rest. Defying this order causes illness. |

**Sleep deprivation** is recognized but not measured in hours. Military surgeons document soldiers collapsing after forced marches. The concept exists; the separate timer doesn't.

**Stimulant interaction:** Well understood empirically. Coffee (*café*) and strong tea (*thé*) prevent sleep. Too much → *surexcitation nerveuse* (nervous overexcitement). Opium (*laudanum*) induces sleep. These are materia medica staples.

### 3.7 Weariness / Exertion System

**CDDA Model:** Activity tracker with exertion deficit, intake offset, weariness levels, recovery requiring sustained low activity.

**1840s: Not a Distinct System**

This is the biggest conceptual gap. CDDA separates weariness (long-term exertion debt) from stamina (short-term burst capacity) and from sleepiness (sleep need). The 1840s lumps all three under **exhaustion/fatigue**.

| CDDA Concept | 1840s Nearest Equivalent | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Weariness tracker | *Fatigue musculaire* (muscular fatigue) | Recognized that muscles tire with use and recover with rest. But not tracked separately from overall exhaustion. |
| Eating reduces weariness | "Nourishment restores strength" | Universally understood. But not as a specific calorie-intake-offsets-exertion-tracker mechanic. Simply: "eating gives you strength back." |
| Intensity-scaled malus (TLG) | "Violent exertion exhausts more than moderate" | Common sense observation, not a mathematical model. |
| Recovery requiring sustained rest | *Repos* (rest) | Yes — rest cures (*cure de repos*) are prescribed. But recovery rate isn't quantified. |

**What does exist:** The concept of **overwork** (*surmenage*) as a cause of disease. Factory workers, soldiers, and washerwomen who work too long develop illness attributed to exhaustion of their vital force. This is a major public health concern in 1840s Paris, intertwined with class politics.

### 3.8 Stamina System

**CDDA Model:** Integer pool, depletes with actions, regenerates at rest. Max = base + cardiofit scaling.

**1840s Replacement: Vital Force / Nervous Energy**

| CDDA Concept | 1840s Term | Fr. Term | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stamina pool | *Force* / *vigueur* | *force*, *vigueur* | Qualitative assessment. "He is strong" or "she is weak." |
| Max stamina | *Constitution* strength | *force de constitution* | Innate + developed through exercise and good nourishment |
| Cardiofit (cardiovascular fitness) | **No concept.** | — | Heart-lung fitness as a measurable variable doesn't exist. The heart is understood as a pump (Harvey, 1628) but "cardiovascular fitness" is a 20th-century concept. |
| Stamina regeneration | Rest restores force | *le repos restaure les forces* | Universal understanding |
| Winded debuff (stamina = 0) | *Essoufflement*, *suffocation* | *essoufflement* | Recognizable breathlessness from overexertion |
| Stim boost to regen | Coffee/stimulants provide temporary energy | *excitation artificielle* | Well known. But understood as borrowing force, not creating it. "Coffee stimulates but does not nourish" (*le café excite mais ne nourrit pas*). |

**Competing explanatory models:**

| Framework | What "Stamina" Is |
|---|---|
| Vitalist | Quantity of *force vitale* available for voluntary action |
| Materialist (emerging) | Chemical energy from food combustion, stored in muscles and blood |
| Humoral (folk) | Balance of blood — sanguine types have more vigor |
| Religious | God-given strength; prayer restores it |
| Mesmerist | Magnetic fluid circulating through the body; blockages cause weakness |

### 3.9 Body Temperature System

**CDDA Model:** 12 body parts, each tracking current/target/frostbite. Equilibrium from ~11 inputs. Constants from 28°C (freezing) to 46°C (scorching).

**1840s: Animal Heat + Fever Taxonomy**

This is one of the most interesting remappings because the 1840s has rich theory about temperature but **cannot measure it precisely**.

**Internal heat generation (*chaleur animale*):**

| Modern Understanding | 1840s Understanding |
|---|---|
| Metabolic heat from cellular respiration | "Combustion in the lungs" (Lavoisier, 1780s). Air's *oxygen* combines with carbon in the blood → heat + CO₂. This is **well established** but the details are wrong (heat is generated throughout the body, not just the lungs). |
| Per-body-part temperature variation | Not measurable. Physicians know extremities get cold first but can't quantify it. |
| Equilibrium from clothing, environment, BMI, etc. | **Qualitatively well understood.** Clothing insulates (*protège contre le froid*). Fat insulates. Wind chills. All recognized. Just not calculated. |

**Fever (*fièvre*) — a disease, not just a number:**

In CDDA, high body temperature is a mechanical state with threshold effects. In the 1840s, fever is **an independent disease category** with its own elaborate taxonomy:

| Fever Type | Fr. Term | Modern Equivalent | 1840s Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intermittent fever | *Fièvre intermittente* | Malaria | Comes and goes in cycles (quotidian, tertian, quartan). Treated with *quinine* (from Peruvian bark / *quinquina*). |
| Continued fever | *Fièvre continue* | Typhoid, typhus | Sustained high fever without remission. Often fatal. |
| Eruptive fever | *Fièvre éruptive* | Measles, smallpox, scarlet fever | Fever + skin eruption. Each has distinctive pattern. |
| Puerperal fever | *Fièvre puerpérale* | Postpartum sepsis | Kills new mothers in hospitals. Semmelweis (1847) will identify hand-washing; not yet accepted. |
| Nervous fever | *Fièvre nerveuse* | Typhoid / any febrile delirium | Fever with mental symptoms |
| Inflammatory fever | *Fièvre inflammatoire* | Various infections | Broussais: all fever = organ inflammation. Fading view. |
| Essential fever | *Fièvre essentielle* | — | Pinel's concept: fever as a disease in itself, without localized lesion. **Major debate:** Do essential fevers exist, or does all fever have an anatomical seat? By the 1840s, the anatomo-clinical school says "no essential fevers" — all have localization. |

**Frostbite (*gelure*, *engelure*):**

Well understood from military medicine (Napoleonic campaigns, Crimean War 1854–56). Larrey's observations are standard teaching. Maps directly to CDDA's frostbite system:

| CDDA Stage | 1840s Term | Fr. Term |
|---|---|---|
| Frostnip | Chilblain / First-degree frostbite | *engelure* (1er degré) |
| Light frostbite | Second-degree frostbite | *gelure* (2e degré) |
| Severe frostbite | Third-degree / Gangrene | *gelure gangréneuse* (3e degré) |

**Clothing and warmth:** The 1840s has a sophisticated practical understanding. Military medicine quantifies clothing needs for campaigns. Civilian medicine prescribes flannel (*flanelle*) next to the skin for those with weak constitutions.

### 3.10 Lifestyle ("Healthy") Stat

**CDDA Model:** Hidden -200 to +200 score from diet/exercise/body composition. Affects max HP, healing, disease resistance.

**1840s Equivalent: Constitution & Hygiène**

This is the system that maps **most naturally** to 1840s thinking, because the concept of long-term health shaped by habits is central to the period's medicine.

| CDDA Concept | 1840s Equivalent | Fr. Term |
|---|---|---|
| Lifestyle stat | *Constitution acquise* (acquired constitution) | As opposed to *constitution native* (innate) |
| `daily_health` (short-term fluctuation) | *État de santé actuel* (current health state) | Assessed at each visit |
| Positive inputs (good food, exercise) | **Hygiène** (in its original, broad sense) | *Hygiène* means "the art of preserving health" — diet, exercise, air, water, sleep, emotions |
| Negative inputs (junk food, drugs, overweight) | *Causes débilitantes* (debilitating causes) | Intemperance, bad air, sloth, excess |
| Max HP effect | *Robustesse* (robustness) | Strong constitution resists injury |
| Healing rate effect | *Pouvoir réparateur* (reparative power) | Good constitution heals faster |
| Disease resistance | *Résistance aux maladies* | Central concept; the whole point of good *hygiène* |

**The Six Non-Naturals (*les six choses non-naturelles*):**

This ancient framework, still taught in the 1840s, defines the six categories of modifiable health inputs:

| Non-Natural | Latin | Fr. Term | CDDA Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Air | *Aer* | *L'air* | Environment / temperature |
| 2. Food & drink | *Cibus et potus* | *Aliments et boissons* | Food system, thirst |
| 3. Motion & rest | *Motus et quies* | *Mouvement et repos* | Activity level, weariness, stamina |
| 4. Sleep & waking | *Somnus et vigilia* | *Sommeil et veille* | Sleep system |
| 5. Excretions & retentions | *Excreta et secreta* | *Excrétions et rétentions* | Digestion output (not modeled in CDDA) |
| 6. Passions of the soul | *Affectus animi* | *Passions de l'âme* | Morale system |

This maps almost perfectly to CDDA's system inputs. The six non-naturals are essentially the game's survival systems, viewed through an ancient lens.

### 3.11 Morale System

**CDDA Model:** Morale points with type, value, decay, duration. Food morale, environmental morale, personality multipliers.

**1840s: Passions, Temperament, and Moral Hygiene**

| CDDA Morale Category | 1840s Category | Fr. Term | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food (good/bad) | Pleasure/disgust of eating | *Plaisir / dégoût de la table* | Gastric sympathy — the stomach communicates pleasure or distress to the whole organism via nerves |
| Food monotony | Dietary monotony | *Monotonie du régime* | Known contributor to scurvy and general debility. Military and naval medicine tracks this. |
| Temperature stress (cold/hot) | Physical suffering from exposure | *Souffrance physique* | Direct cause of disease and moral depression |
| Wet / dried off | Discomfort | *Incommodité* | Minor but recognized |
| Addiction craving | Habitual need / Withdrawal | *Besoin habituel*, *sevrage* | Well documented for opium (*opiumisme*), alcohol (*alcoolisme*), tobacco (*tabagisme*). Understood as acquired need. |
| Personality multipliers | **Temperament** | *Tempérament* | This is where 1840s is richest: |

**Temperament as morale multiplier:**

| CDDA Trait | 1840s Temperament | Fr. Term | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Optimistic | Sanguine | *Tempérament sanguin* | Warm, hopeful, resilient, quick to recover spirits |
| Bad Tempered | Choleric | *Tempérament colérique / bilieux* | Irritable, intense, quick to anger, sustained resentment |
| (no direct equivalent) | Melancholic | *Tempérament mélancolique* | Prone to sadness, introspection, slow to recover from setbacks |
| (no direct equivalent) | Phlegmatic | *Tempérament flegmatique / lymphatique* | Calm, slow, indifferent, hard to rouse in either direction |
| Numb | *Apathie* | *Apathie* | Recognized pathological state; emotional flatness |

**"Moral causes" of disease (*causes morales*):**

The 1840s takes seriously what modern medicine would call psychosomatic effects, but goes further — emotions are **primary causes** of organic disease:

| Passion | Fr. Term | Believed Physical Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Grief | *Chagrin* | Weakens constitution, invites tuberculosis, causes wasting |
| Fright / Terror | *Frayeur*, *terreur* | Sudden death (heart stops), paralysis, madness |
| Anger | *Colère* | Apoplexy (stroke), liver disease, bilious fever |
| Nostalgia (homesickness) | *Nostalgie* | **A recognized medical diagnosis.** Military disease. Causes wasting, fever, death in displaced persons. Documented since 17th century. |
| Prolonged anxiety | *Inquiétude prolongée* | Gastric disease, nervous exhaustion, insomnia |
| Joy (excessive) | *Joie excessive* | Sudden death (rare, but in the literature) |
| Jealousy | *Jalousie* | Liver disease ("jaundice from jealousy") |
| Shame | *Honte* | Wasting, mental derangement |

**Traitement moral (moral treatment):** Philippe Pinel developed *traitement moral* for insanity — treatment designed to act on "the passions, sentiments, and emotions." His student Esquirol held that insanity and the "moral affections" that cause it concern body, mind, and morality together. This is not "moral" in the ethical sense but in the psychological sense: treating the patient's emotional and mental state as a therapeutic intervention. This maps to CDDA's morale system having mechanical effects on health.

**Nostalgia (*nostalgie*)** deserves special note: it is a fully medicalized diagnosis, with case studies, proposed treatments (return home, distraction, tonics), and documented fatalities. It maps to nothing in CDDA but would be a natural extension for morale in a displacement/survival context.

### 3.12 Stimulant System

**CDDA Model:** Simple integer `stim`, decays toward 0, positive delays sleep and boosts stamina regen, >250 = heart attack, <-200 = lethal withdrawal.

**1840s: Materia Medica of Excitants and Sedatives**

This is one of the **best-mapped** systems. The 1840s has excellent empirical knowledge of stimulants and sedatives, even without understanding their chemistry.

| CDDA Range | 1840s Concept | Fr. Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Positive stim | Excitation | *Excitation*, *stimulation* | The body is energized beyond its natural state. |
| Stim = 0 | Natural state | *État naturel* | Baseline |
| Negative stim | Collapse / Withdrawal | *Collapsus*, *sevrage* | Post-stimulant crash, or absence of habitual stimulant |
| Stim > 250 (heart attack) | Overdose / Poisoning | *Empoisonnement*, *surdose* | Well documented for opium, belladonna, strychnine. Heart failure from excess recognized. |
| Stim < -200 (lethal) | Fatal withdrawal | *Sevrage mortel* | Opium withdrawal deaths recognized. Alcohol withdrawal (*delirium tremens*) described by Thomas Sutton (1813). |

**Key substances and their classification:**

| Substance | Fr. Term | Category | Medical Use | Abuse Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coffee (*Coffea*) | *Café* | Excitant (*excitant*) | Prescribed for drowsiness, headache, asthma | Overuse → *surexcitation nerveuse* |
| Alcohol (ethanol) | *Boissons alcooliques* | Excitant (small dose) / Narcotic (large dose) | Prescribed as tonic, warming agent, surgical anesthetic | *Alcoolisme chronique* (coined 1849) |
| Opium / Laudanum | *Opium*, *laudanum* | Narcotic (*narcotique*) | Pain relief, sleep induction, diarrhea treatment (cholera!) | *Opiumisme* — severe dependence |
| Tobacco | *Tabac* | Excitant / Narcotic | Prescribed as fumigation for intestinal ailments | *Tabagisme* — habitual use |
| Ether | *Éther* | Excitant / Anesthetic | Anesthetic use begins 1846 (Morton's demonstration). Revolution! | *Éthéromanie* — recreational inhalation |
| Camphor | *Camphre* | Excitant / Anti-spasmodic | Raspail's universal remedy. Also used against cholera. | Respiratory irritant in excess |
| Strychnine | *Strychnine* | Excitant (tiny dose) / Poison | "Tonic" in minute doses for paralysis, debility | Fatal in slightly higher doses |
| Mercury | *Mercure* | Alterant (*altérant*) | Syphilis treatment (calomel). Universal "alterative." | Mercury poisoning (*hydrargyrisme*) |
| Quinine | *Quinine* (from *quinquina*) | Febrifuge (*fébrifuge*) | Specific against intermittent fever (malaria). One of the few genuinely effective drugs. | Cinchonism (tinnitus, deafness) from excess |

**The excitant/sedative spectrum** is the 1840s version of CDDA's stim axis:

```
SEDATIVE ←————————— NATURAL STATE ——————————→ EXCITANT
(narcotique)          (état naturel)            (excitant/stimulant)

opium, ether,         health                    coffee, alcohol (small),
belladonna,                                     strychnine (tiny),
henbane                                         camphor
```

## Part IV: Cross-System Interactions Through the 1840s Lens

### 4.1 The 1840s Interaction Web

Where CDDA models discrete system interactions (activity → calorie burn → weight → temperature), the 1840s sees a **holistic web** mediated by three channels:

| Channel | Fr. Term | What It Carries | CDDA Systems It Connects |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nervous system** | *Système nerveux* | Commands, sensations, "nervous fluid" | Sleep, stamina, morale, stimulants, pain |
| **Blood / Circulation** | *Sang*, *circulation* | Nourishment, heat, vital force | Digestion → body storage, temperature, vitamin effects |
| **Sympathies** (*sympathies*) | *Sympathies organiques* | Organ-to-organ communication via nerves | Stomach ↔ brain (hunger affects mood), skin ↔ internal organs (cold exposure → internal disease) |

**"Sympathies" is the key concept CDDA lacks.** In 1840s medicine, organs affect each other at a distance through nerve connections. Examples:
- Stomach distress causes headache (gastric sympathy)
- Cold feet cause a chest cold (cutaneous-pulmonary sympathy)
- Emotional shock stops digestion (cerebro-gastric sympathy)
- Fright causes miscarriage (cerebro-uterine sympathy)

This creates a **denser interaction web** than CDDA models, but with less mechanical precision.

### 4.2 Equivalent of CDDA's Cross-System Diagram

```
NOURISHMENT ──→ Blood Quality ──→ Constitution ──→ Disease Resistance
     │                │                 │
     ├──→ Body Heat   │                 ├──→ Embonpoint / Cachexie
     │                │                 │
     └──→ Force       │                 └──→ Healing Power
                      │
EXERTION ──→ Nervous Exhaustion ──→ Need for Sleep
     │              │
     ├──→ Muscular Fatigue          └──→ Susceptibility to Passions
     │
     └──→ Thirst (activity increases thirst — observed)

PASSIONS ──→ Gastric Sympathy ──→ Digestion Quality
     │                              │
     ├──→ Nervous Agitation         └──→ Nourishment
     │
     └──→ Direct Organ Disease (via moral cause)

ENVIRONMENT ──→ Air Quality ──→ Miasmatic Disease
     │              │
     ├──→ Cold/Heat  └──→ Constitution (hardening vs weakening)
     │
     └──→ Humidity ──→ Rheumatism, Chest Disease
```

### 4.3 Interaction: What the Different Practitioners See

The same cross-system interaction looks different to each practitioner type:

**Patient is: exhausted, hungry, cold, depressed, and developing a cough.**

| Practitioner | Diagnosis | Fr. Diagnosis | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Academic physician | *Débilité générale* with incipient bronchitis from cold exposure + poor nourishment | *Débilité générale avec bronchite naissante* | Nutritious diet (broths, meat), warmth, rest, possibly *sirop de tolu* (cough syrup) |
| Broussais follower (declining) | Gastric inflammation spreading to lungs via sympathy | *Gastro-entérite avec irritation pulmonaire* | Leeches to abdomen, bland diet, rest |
| Vitalist | Vital force depleted by cold and want; respiratory organs losing vitality | *Épuisement de la force vitale* | Tonics (*toniques*): wine, quinine, iron. Warmth. |
| Homeopath | Totality of symptoms matches a specific remedy picture | — | Single remedy in infinitesimal dose (possibly *Arsenicum album* for this symptom picture) |
| Raspailist | Parasites taking hold in weakened body | *Parasites* | Camphor: cigarettes, lozenges, rubbing alcohol |
| Catholic healer | Spiritual trial; body weakened by moral suffering | *Épreuve divine* | Prayer to patron saint of the sick (St. Camillus), blessed candle, holy water, possibly pilgrimage |
| Marabout | *Jinn* possession or evil eye (*'ayn*) weakening the body | عين / جن | Quranic recitation, *ruqya* (spiritual healing), herbal preparations, protective amulets (*ta'wiz* / *hirz*) |
| Folk healer | "Caught cold" (*pris froid*); the cold entered the body | *A pris froid* | Hot herbal tisane (thyme, elderflower), poultice on chest, mustard foot-bath (*bain de pieds sinapisé*), keep warm |

## Part VII: Implementation Notes

### 7.1 Refluffing Strategy Summary

The refluffing is **not** a find-and-replace of terms. It requires structural changes to how information is presented:

| Principle | Implementation |
|---|---|
| **Quantitative → Qualitative** | Replace numerical displays (calories, vitamin levels, temperature in degrees) with descriptive tiers using period vocabulary |
| **Separate → Merged** | Present stamina + weariness as one "Force" (*force*) system with different descriptions for acute vs chronic depletion |
| **Unified → Split** | Present digestion as four stages (coction → chylification → sanguification → assimilation) even if the underlying mechanics are still two-compartment |
| **Explained → Mysterious** | Vitamin deficiencies appear as named diseases without explanation. "You are developing scurvy" not "Your vitamin C is low" |
| **Measured → Observed** | Temperature displays by touch-based assessment: "your hands are ice-cold" not "hands: 28°C" |
| **Objective → Practitioner-dependent** | Different healer types give different diagnoses for the same state |
